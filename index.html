Sunlight Zone (0 - 200m)

What is the Sunlight Zone?


The Sunlight Zone is the uppermost layer of the ocean, extending from the surface to about 200 meters deep. It is well-lit by sunlight, allowing photosynthesis to occur, which supports a diverse range of marine life.


Home to most marine life, including dolphins, turtles and coral reefs.

Twilight Zone (200 - 1000m)

What is the Twilight Zone?


The Twilight Zone receives very little sunlight and spans from 200 to 1000 meters deep. It’s a mysterious region where sunlight starts to fade and many creatures exhibit bioluminescence.


Inhabitants include squids, hatchetfish, and lanternfish, adapted to low-light conditions.

Keep scrolling to enter the deeper layers of the ocean.

Midnight Zone (1000 - 4000m)

What is the Midnight Zone?


This pitch-black layer extends from 1000 to 4000 meters. No sunlight reaches here. Temperatures are near freezing and pressures are immense.


Species here include anglerfish and viperfish, which use bioluminescence to lure prey or communicate in the dark.

The deep sea is getting colder and darker... scroll on!

Abyssal Zone (4000 - 6000m)

What is the Abyssal Zone?


This zone extends from 4000 to 6000 meters. It's one of the least explored environments on Earth, with total darkness, crushing pressure, and near-freezing temperatures.


Creatures like the Dumbo Octopus and sea cucumbers survive in this harsh environment.

Approaching the ocean’s deepest secrets…

Hadal Zone (6000 - 11000m)

What is the Hadal Zone?


The Hadal Zone includes ocean trenches and the deepest parts of the sea, like the Mariana Trench, reaching depths of up to 11,000 meters.


Despite the extreme pressure, life exists here—like the ghostly snailfish and tiny amphipods.
